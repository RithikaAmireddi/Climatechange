Understanding
Climate Change

Climate change refers to a long-term alteration in weather patterns that define a particular location. It is measured over decades or even centuries, unlike the short-term changes in weather that we experience daily. While climate change has always been a natural phenomenon, its current pace is much faster due to human activities.

The burning of fossil fuels, such as coal, oil, and gas, releases greenhouse gases, particularly carbon dioxide, into the atmosphere. These gases act like a blanket, trapping heat from the sun and causing the planet to warm up.

Climate change is happening at an alarming rate, driven by human activity. Burning fossil fuels releases greenhouse gases that trap heat, causing global temperatures to rise faster than ever before in the past 2,000 years. The Earth's surface is now 1.1Â°C warmer than pre-industrial times, and the last decade was the hottest on record. This warming isn't just about temperature - it's causing a cascade of effects like droughts, floods, wildfires, and rising sea levels, threatening our planet's health.

The consequences of climate change are significant, and the Earth's climate is already changing. The severity of this change depends on the amount of greenhouse gas emissions we keep emitting. If we significantly reduce emissions, we can limit global warming. However, without major reductions, we could face several degrees of warming by the end of this century.

This rapid warming is unlike anything Earth has experienced before, and the evidence is clear - temperatures are rising, sea levels are climbing, and glaciers are melting. These trends are confirmed by independent research all over the world. The rise in temperature has caused extreme weather patterns such as heatwaves, droughts, floods, and hurricanes. These changes will have a significant impact on our food and water supplies, our health, and our economies.

The bottom line is that Earth's climate is changing at an alarming rate, primarily due to human actions. However, we can still slow down this process and protect our planet for future generations by taking steps to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

Climate change isn't a slow burn anymore - it's a raging fire. The rapid escalation of extreme weather events like wildfires, hurricanes, and heatwaves is shocking.

The culprit behind this climate chaos? Burning fossil fuels. These activities are pumping greenhouse gases into the atmosphere at an alarming rate. These gases act like a heat trap, causing global temperatures to soar.

The evidence is undeniable. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), a leading international scientific body, has concluded that human activity is the main driver of the observed warming since the mid-20th century.

**Facts:**

  • Global temperatures have risen by roughly 2 degrees Fahrenheit (1 degree Celsius) since the late 19th century, coinciding with the rise in greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Ice core data paints a worrying picture - glaciers and ice sheets are melting at alarming rates, contributing to rising sea levels.
  • Over 90% of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases is absorbed by the oceans, disrupting marine ecosystems and currents.
  • Extreme weather events like heatwaves, droughts, floods, and wildfires are becoming more frequent and severe, exactly as climate models predicted.

This isn't just a climate shift; it's a human-caused crisis. We need global cooperation, aggressive emissions reduction strategies, and adaptation measures to mitigate the worst impacts.

More extreme weather events

Heat waves, droughts, floods, and storms are expected to become more common and intense. This is already being observed around the world, and is expected to worsen as the planet warms.

Alarm Bells Ringing: Climate Change Accelerates

As the Earth warms, ice sheets and glaciers melt, causing sea levels to rise. This threatens coastal communities around the world, and could displace millions of people.

Changes in precipitation patterns

Some areas will experience more frequent and intense rainfall, while others will become drier. This can lead to water shortages, agricultural disruptions, and ecosystem damage.

Ocean acidification

The oceans absorb about a quarter of the carbon dioxide released into the atmosphere. This makes the oceans more acidic, which harms marine life, including shellfish, coral reefs, and plankton.

Loss of biodiversity

Climate change is already causing species to move to cooler areas or adapt to changing environments. However, some species will not be able to adapt and will become extinct.
